Introduction

The NBA playoffs represent the highest level of competition in professional basketball, where teams compete in a series of elimination rounds to determine the league champ. For our analysis we decided to take a look at historical NBA regular-season level data and see how different performance metrics (offensive and defensive) influence a teams’ success in the playoffs. With the increasing availability of advanced basketball statistics, it is now more possible than ever to examine which team characteristics are most strongly associated with success in the playoffs. The purpose of this analysis is to identify key factors of success in the playoffs, as well as use those factors to develop a model capable of predicting the winner of a playoff series given the relevant teams’ performance in the regular season.

Methods

Exploratory Data Analysis

To identify which regular season team statistics were most strongly associated with playoff success, an exploratory data analysis (EDA) was conducted using data from NBA playoff teams over the ten most recent completed seasons (2015-2024). A ten-year period was selected to provide a sufficiently large sample size while maintaining relevance to the modern NBA. The style of play in professional basketball has evolved considerably over time, particularly with the increased emphasis on three-point shooting and offensive efficiency. Restricting the analysis to the past decade helps ensure that observed relationships reflect contemporary trends rather than outdated strategies.

Ten regular season team performance metrics were selected based on their prevalence in basketball analytics literature and their potential relationship with playoff success. These metrics included Win Percentage (Win%), Net Rating, Offensive Rating, Defensive Rating, Simple Rating System (SRS), Effective Field Goal Percentage (eFG%), True Shooting Percentage (TS%), Three-point Attempt Rate (3PAr), Turnover Percentage (TOV%), and Offensive Rebound Percentage (ORB%).

Playoff success was measured using playoff win percentage. For each metric, the Pearson correlation coefficient was calculated to quantify the strength and direction of the linear relationship between the regular season statistic and playoff win percentage. Pearson correlation was chosen because it provides an interpretable measure of association between continuous variable and serves as a useful first step in identifying potentially important predictors.

Results

Model Performance and Evaluation

To determine the most robust predictive framework for the NBA playoffs, we evaluated both an Elastic Net Logistic Regression and a Random Forest classifier. Performance was assessed using accuracy and the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (roc_auc). Because playoff series outcomes are fundamentally driven by probabilistic edges where a team must win four out of seven games, we prioritized roc_auc to measure how effectively each model separates historical winners from losers across varying decision thresholds.

The Random Forest model demonstrated superior predictive power, achieving a higher roc_auc than the Elastic Net Logistic Regression. While logistic regression struggles with the highly correlated, multi-collinear nature of team basketball metrics, the Random Forest model naturally accommodated non-linear relationships and captured distinct playoff intensity thresholds.

Feature Importance

As shown by the plot, the logistic regression model selected only eight of the predictors included in the recipe. Among the top five of those include: Win%, True-shooting%, Effective Field-Goal%, and Offensive/Net rating (all of which are differentials between Team A and Team B). This means that the model prioritizes teams with high regular season win/loss records (to be expected), as well as efficient shooting, more so than it does defensive metrics. These results highlight the distinct shift the NBA has taken in the modern era, putting a heavy importance on a teams’ ability to score rather than defend. Interestingly enough, the model also selected the head-to-head Win% differential statistic, implying that a teams’ regular season win/loss record against a specific team yields predictive power in the outcome of a series matchup in the playoffs.

The Random Forest yields somewhat similar relevant features. Among the top five of those include: Win%, Effective Field-Goal%, True-Shooting%, Plus-Minus, and Net rating (once again, all of which are differentials between Team A and B). We can see that both models yield the same top three predictors yet in slight different orders; the random forest model prioritizes Effective Field-Goal% over True-Shooting%. Although these differences are slight, this means that the logistic regression model puts a heavier emphasis on points generated via the free-throw (included in ts%), than the random forest model does.
